# Institutional Immune Systems: A Transdisciplinary Framework for Defending Epistemic Infrastructure in the Age of Generative AI

**A Proposal for University-Led Operational Research and Development**

---

**Working Paper — March 2026**

**Abstract**

The rapid proliferation of generative artificial intelligence has introduced an unprecedented volume of synthetic content into the global information ecosystem. Unlike prior disruptions to information integrity, the marginal cost of producing convincing synthetic text, audio, image, and video content has collapsed to near zero, fundamentally altering the asymmetry between content production and content verification. This paper argues that the primary societal structures under threat by the proliferation of AI-generated content are not individual consumers of information but rather the *institutional* infrastructure — educational systems, libraries, local governments, and professional credentialing bodies — upon which democratic societies depend for shared epistemics. Drawing on emerging evidence from studies of AI-generated academic fraud, cognitive offloading, synthetic public comment flooding, and publishing ecosystem contamination, we propose a university-centered, multi-disciplinary operational research and development framework designed to be deployed, measured, and iteratively improved. The framework positions the host university not merely as a site of analysis but as an *operational hub* coordinating adaptive institutional defense across its surrounding community. We present this framework as an explicitly incomplete starting point — a version 1.0 architecture designed to evolve through structured feedback loops, cross-institutional learning, and empirical validation. Concrete deliverables are proposed for every major arm of a higher education institution, from research centers to extension services, aligned with both the urgency of the crisis and the institutional incentives necessary for sustained commitment.

**Keywords:** generative AI, epistemic infrastructure, institutional resilience, information integrity, multi-disciplinary research, cognitive offloading, content provenance, democratic governance, adaptive frameworks

## 1. Introduction: The Epistemic Crisis as Institutional Crisis

The information environment has entered a period of structural transformation that demands both scholarly analysis and operational response. Since the public release of large language models in late 2022, the volume of AI-generated content has surged across every domain of public discourse. An October 2025 report found that AI-generated content had overtaken the quantity of human-made content by November 2024 and reached an estimated 52% of all online content by May 2025 (European Parliamentary Research Service, 2025). This is not merely a quantitative increase; it represents a qualitative shift in the relationship between content production costs and verification costs that has no precedent in the history of mass communication.

The prevailing discourse around this crisis tends to frame it in individualist terms: how can *people* learn to detect AI-generated content? How can *consumers* protect themselves from misinformation? This framing, while not wrong, is dangerously incomplete. The deeper threat is institutional. Democratic societies do not depend on each individual citizen independently verifying every claim they encounter. They depend on *institutions* — schools that cultivate critical thinking, libraries that curate reliable knowledge, governments that process authentic public input, and professional bodies that certify genuine expertise — to maintain the epistemic commons upon which collective decision-making rests.

These institutions are now under simultaneous stress from generative AI, and most are responding with either paralysis or naive adoption. The present paper argues that this institutional vulnerability represents both the most consequential dimension of the AI-generated content crisis and the most actionable intervention point. Specifically, we propose that a university is uniquely positioned to serve as the operational hub for a coordinated, multi-institutional, multi-disciplinary response — and that doing so represents a strategic opportunity of the first order for any institution willing to lead.

### 1.1 The Asymmetry Problem

Previous threats to information integrity — propaganda, yellow journalism, spam, state-sponsored disinformation — were constrained by production costs. Generating convincing disinformation at scale required organized human labor: writers, translators, social media operatives, graphic designers. The cost structure imposed natural limits on volume and required the backing of well-resourced actors (Benkler et al., 2018).

Generative AI has effectively removed this constraint. A single individual with access to commercially available tools can now produce a volume of convincing synthetic content — text, images, audio, and video — that would have previously required a state-level operation. This is not a difference in degree; it is a difference in kind. The asymmetry between the cost of generating noise and the cost of verifying signal has widened to a degree that threatens the viability of institutional mechanisms designed for a fundamentally different information environment.

### 1.2 The Scope of Contamination

The evidence base for the breadth of this contamination is growing rapidly across multiple domains:

**Academic publishing.** A study published in *Nature Human Behaviour* analyzing over one million scientific papers found a sharp uptick in LLM-modified content beginning just months after ChatGPT's release, with approximately one in five computer science papers and one in seven biomedical abstracts showing signs of AI assistance by mid-2024 (Liang et al., 2025). Separately, researchers documented a surge of low-quality papers exploiting public health datasets, with formulaic studies increasing from an average of four per year to 190 in 2024 alone (Spick et al., 2025; reported in *Science*).

**Publishing and information supply chains.** Amazon's Kindle Direct Publishing platform has been flooded with AI-generated books, with estimates ranging from 10,000 to 40,000 new AI-generated titles per month (eReadersForum, 2025). The contamination extends across publishing platforms and into library acquisition pipelines, with particular concern about AI-generated nonfiction that presents fabricated information as authoritative — including at least one documented case of an AI-generated mushroom identification guide that listed poisonous varieties as safe (CBC News, 2024).

**Democratic processes.** The U.S. House of Representatives passed the Comment Integrity and Management Act of 2024 in response to growing concern about AI-generated comments flooding federal regulatory proceedings (Nextgov, 2024). The bill's sponsor noted that the measure aimed to ensure "every comment submitted by electronic means comes from a real person, not an automated program." The FCC has pursued parallel rulemaking on AI-generated robocalls and the use of deepfakes in political advertising (FCC, 2024).

**Cognitive capacity.** A 2025 MIT Media Lab study found that participants who used LLMs for essay writing over four months "consistently underperformed at neural, linguistic, and behavioral levels" compared to those who wrote without AI assistance, with EEG data showing reduced neural connectivity indicative of cognitive disengagement (Kosmyna et al., 2025). A separate study of 319 knowledge workers found that user confidence in AI was predictive of reduced critical thinking effort (Lee et al., 2025, CHI '25). A mixed-methods study of 666 UK participants documented a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking scores, mediated by cognitive offloading (Gerlich, 2025).

These are not isolated phenomena. They represent the early signals of a systemic transformation in the relationship between human cognition, institutional function, and synthetic content.

### 1.3 The Case for University Leadership

Universities occupy a unique position in the institutional landscape. They are simultaneously research institutions capable of rigorous analysis, educational institutions responsible for developing the next generation's cognitive capacities, community anchors with relationships to local schools, libraries, governments, and professional bodies, and credentialing bodies whose own processes are under direct threat.

No other institution has this breadth of relevant capacity. And yet, most universities have responded to the generative AI moment primarily through narrow policy questions — academic integrity policies, acceptable use guidelines — rather than recognizing the broader strategic opportunity and civic obligation.

This paper proposes a different orientation: the university as *operational hub* for a coordinated defense of epistemic infrastructure across its surrounding institutional ecosystem. This is not a call for a single new research center (though research is a component). It is a proposal for a multi-disciplinary, multi-stakeholder operational initiative that generates deployable methodology, measures outcomes, and iteratively improves — while simultaneously producing the publications, grants, community partnerships, and institutional prestige that university incentive structures reward.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Institutions as Epistemic Infrastructure

### 2.1 The Concept of Epistemic Infrastructure

We use the term "epistemic infrastructure" to refer to the interconnected set of institutions, norms, practices, and technologies through which a society establishes, curates, transmits, and validates shared knowledge. This concept draws on work in social epistemology (Goldman, 1999), the sociology of knowledge institutions (Fricker, 2007), and democratic theory's emphasis on informed citizenry as a precondition for self-governance (Dewey, 1927; Habermas, 1962/1989).

Epistemic infrastructure operates analogously to physical infrastructure: it is largely invisible when functioning, catastrophic when it fails, and extremely costly to rebuild once degraded. Unlike physical infrastructure, however, epistemic infrastructure depends on trust — and trust, once eroded, does not simply return when the pipes are repaired.

### 2.2 The Immune System Analogy

We propose that institutions function as a society's *epistemic immune system*. Schools develop the cognitive capacities (analogous to immune cells) that enable individuals to resist manipulation. Libraries curate and provide access to verified knowledge (analogous to antibody production). Governments process authentic public input and make legitimate collective decisions (analogous to coordinated immune response). Professional credentialing bodies certify genuine expertise (analogous to self/non-self recognition).

Generative AI constitutes a novel pathogen for this immune system — one that the existing defenses were not designed to recognize. The challenge is not merely to develop new defenses but to do so while the infection is active and spreading.

### 2.3 The Window of Institutional Capture

A critical observation motivates the urgency of this proposal: institutional norms, once established, are remarkably persistent. The actors who first establish frameworks, tools, and practices within an institution disproportionately shape its long-term orientation. Currently, most institutions are in a period of confused receptivity — open to guidance but lacking it.

Actors optimizing for extraction (vendors selling AI solutions without adequate safeguards, content farms flooding publishing pipelines, political operatives deploying synthetic astroturfing) are not typically interested in the slow work of institutional norm-setting. This creates a narrow but consequential window in which actors oriented toward institutional resilience can establish frameworks that persist. The closing speed of this window is difficult to estimate but is almost certainly faster than institutional adaptation cycles would normally accommodate.

## 3. Sector Analysis: Threat Landscapes and Intervention Points

The following analysis examines four institutional sectors that constitute the primary components of local epistemic infrastructure. For each, we identify the specific threat landscape created by generative AI, key institutional vulnerabilities, and intervention points accessible through both top-down (policy and leadership) and bottom-up (individual practitioner and community) approaches.

### 3.1 Education (K-12 and Higher Education)

**Threat Landscape.** The surface-level threats — students submitting AI-generated work, the erosion of learning through shortcut-taking — are well-documented but obscure deeper structural dangers. Curriculum and educational materials are increasingly contaminated by AI-generated misinformation that presents with the surface markers of authority. The credentialing function of educational degrees is eroding as employers cannot reliably determine what a graduate independently knows versus what was produced through AI delegation. Research integrity is declining as AI-generated and AI-assisted papers flood academic publishing (Liang et al., 2025). Most critically, the cognitive capacity for deep reading, critical analysis, and slow deliberative thinking — the foundational skills that education exists to develop — appears to be directly undermined by habitual AI use (Kosmyna et al., 2025; Gerlich, 2025).

**Vulnerabilities.** Educators are overwhelmed and under-resourced. Administrators oscillate between blanket bans and uncritical adoption. Revenue dependence creates reluctance to make changes that might appear anti-innovation. Academic publishing's peer review system, already strained, is buckling under synthetic submissions.

**Intervention Architecture.**

*Top-down:* Mandate AI and information literacy as core curricular requirements, treating epistemic fitness with the same urgency previously afforded to computer literacy. Reform assessment methods away from easily-automated outputs toward demonstrated reasoning, oral defense, and process documentation. Establish institutional research integrity standards specifically addressing synthetic content.

*Bottom-up:* Support educators in developing and sharing "epistemic fitness" curricula — practical exercises in source evaluation, adversarial thinking about information, and pattern recognition for synthetic content. Create cross-institutional educator networks for rapid sharing of effective pedagogical approaches.

### 3.2 Libraries and Public Knowledge Infrastructure

**Threat Landscape.** Libraries' core function — connecting communities with reliable information — faces compound pressure. Library catalogs and acquisition pipelines are increasingly contaminated by AI-generated books that present fabricated content as authoritative (NPR, 2024; eReadersForum, 2025). Reference services confront a new challenge: patrons arriving with AI-generated "facts" they believe to be true. The upstream publishing ecosystem on which libraries depend for quality material is itself degrading. Digital literacy programming, offered by many library systems, now requires radical upgrades from "how to use the internet" to "how to navigate an adversarial information environment."

**Vulnerabilities.** Chronic underfunding. Librarians possess high-level curation skills but often lack AI-specific technical training. The profession's philosophical orientation toward openness and access, while ordinarily a strength, creates hesitancy around the filtering and gatekeeping functions now urgently needed. Governance structures are slow-moving and politically constrained.

**Intervention Architecture.**

*Top-down:* Reposition libraries explicitly as "epistemic infrastructure" in policy and funding frameworks — a reframing that justifies expanded mandates and budgets. Develop acquisition standards addressing AI-generated content contamination. Create consortia-level shared resources for AI literacy programming. Advocate for library representation in AI governance policy conversations.

*Bottom-up:* Develop community-facing "information self-defense" workshops leveraging libraries' existing high-trust community relationships. Build partnerships with local schools and journalism outlets to create reinforcing ecosystems of information literacy. Pilot "community verification hub" models where libraries serve as trusted sites for evaluating questionable content.

### 3.3 Local and State Government

**Threat Landscape.** Democratic processes face acute near-term danger. AI-generated public comments are flooding regulatory proceedings, making it impossible to gauge authentic public sentiment — a concern significant enough to prompt bipartisan federal legislation (Comment Integrity and Management Act, 2024). Synthetic constituent communications to elected officials undermine representative feedback loops. Deepfake audio and video targeting local officials operate in environments with far less media infrastructure for debunking than exists at the national level (FCC, 2024). Government agencies are adopting AI tools for service delivery without adequate frameworks for validation, bias assessment, or failure protocols. Citizen disengagement accelerates as the authenticity of public information becomes uncertain.

**Vulnerabilities.** Razor-thin budgets with minimal technical capacity. Heavy dependence on vendors with incentive misalignment. Part-time, under-compensated elected officials making technology decisions based primarily on vendor pitches. Public comment and input mechanisms designed for a world where submission required a human author.

**Intervention Architecture.**

*Top-down:* Develop authentication frameworks for public comment and constituent communication that create verified channels alongside open ones. Create model AI procurement standards for local government adoption, covering bias auditing, transparency, failure protocols, and sunset clauses. Require AI impact assessments before deployment in public-facing services.

*Bottom-up:* Equip citizens to attend local government meetings and raise AI governance questions. Mobilize civic technologists as informal advisors to local boards. Form local civic AI working groups bridging technical understanding and democratic governance. Support local journalism covering municipal AI adoption.

### 3.4 Professional Associations and Credentialing Bodies

**Threat Landscape.** When AI systems can pass professional licensing examinations, the meaning of professional credentials requires fundamental reexamination. Professional knowledge bases — journals, continuing education, best-practice guidelines — face the same contamination dynamics as consumer information, but with higher stakes, as practitioners make consequential decisions based on professional literature. Ethical and practice standards have not kept pace: questions of standard-of-care, malpractice, and professional duty in AI-augmented practice are largely unresolved.

**Vulnerabilities.** Consensus-driven governance structures that move at multi-year timescales. Leadership cohorts whose professional instincts were formed pre-AI. Revenue models dependent on existing credentialing processes. Smaller professions lack resources for independent AI-related adaptation. The deepest vulnerability is philosophical: the entire credentialing model assumes the human is the primary agent doing the work, an assumption increasingly at odds with practice.

**Intervention Architecture.**

*Top-down:* Redesign credentialing assessments to test what matters in an AI-augmented world — clinical judgment, ethical reasoning, critical evaluation of AI outputs, and recognition of tool limitations. Establish AI-specific ethical guidelines with concrete standards for disclosure, verification, and professional duty. Create professional literature integrity standards and cross-profession coalitions for shared framework development.

*Bottom-up:* Support practitioners in raising AI governance issues within professional communities. Build evidence bases of real-world cases where AI created professional risk. Develop mentorship networks focused on the judgment and relational skills that constitute irreducible human professional value. Enable independent practitioner groups to set de facto standards through practice norms when official bodies lag.

## 4. The Adaptive Framework: Architecture for Self-Improvement

A critical design requirement for any institutional response to generative AI is adaptability. The threat landscape is evolving faster than any static framework can accommodate. The proposed framework therefore incorporates structured mechanisms for self-improvement at every level.

### 4.1 The Observation-Orientation-Decision-Action (OODA) Adaptation Cycle

Borrowing from decision theory (Boyd, 1987), the framework operates on continuous OODA cycles at three temporal scales:

**Tactical (weekly-monthly):** Individual pilot programs and interventions collect structured data on effectiveness — workshop attendance, participant feedback, behavioral change indicators, institutional policy adoption rates. This data feeds rapid iteration of specific tools and approaches.

**Operational (quarterly):** Cross-sector working groups review tactical data, identify emerging patterns, and adjust sector-specific strategies. Failed approaches are documented with the same rigor as successes. The *failure documentation* component is critical — it is how the framework learns what does not work, which is at least as valuable as learning what does.

**Strategic (annually):** A comprehensive framework review assesses structural assumptions, evaluates whether the threat landscape has shifted in ways that require architectural changes, and incorporates new research findings. This review is designed to produce a versioned update to the framework itself — explicitly acknowledging that Version 1.0 (this document) is incomplete and expected to be superseded.

### 4.2 Feedback Mechanisms

The framework mandates specific feedback channels:

- **Practitioner reporting:** Standardized templates for educators, librarians, government staff, and professionals to document what they observe in their institutions — what threats are materializing, what interventions are working, what gaps exist.
- **Community sensing:** Structured mechanisms for community members to report emerging patterns — new types of synthetic content, novel manipulation tactics, institutional failures or successes.
- **Academic integration:** A commitment to publishing findings (including null results) in peer-reviewed venues, creating a scholarly feedback loop that subjects the framework's assumptions and methods to external scrutiny.
- **Cross-institutional benchmarking:** As additional universities and communities adopt or adapt the framework, structured data sharing enables comparative analysis and accelerated learning.

### 4.3 Versioning and Transparency

The framework is explicitly versioned. Each iteration documents what changed, why it changed, what evidence motivated the change, and what remains uncertain. This transparency serves dual purposes: it enables other institutions to learn from the evolution, and it models the epistemic humility that the framework seeks to cultivate in the institutions it serves.

## 5. University-Centered Deliverables: Aligning Mission with Incentive

The following deliverables are designed to engage every major arm of a research university, aligned with both the public mission and the institutional incentive structures — publication records, grant funding, community impact metrics, transdisciplinary prestige, and competitive differentiation — that drive sustained commitment.

### 5.1 Research

**Deliverable 1: Epistemic Resilience Research Center (ERRC).** An transdisciplinary center drawing faculty from computer science (detection, provenance and learning technologies), data science (bringing together compute, the data lifecycle, and math/stats) information science (library and knowledge systems), political science (democratic governance under information stress), psychology and cognitive science (cognitive offloading, critical thinking development), education (pedagogy for epistemic fitness), communications (media literacy, journalism), philosophy (epistemology, ethics of AI), and relevant professional schools (law, medicine, public health, business). The center's research agenda is structured around the framework's adaptation cycles, ensuring that scholarship directly informs deployed methodology and vice versa.

*Incentive alignment:* Transdisciplinary research centers are high-prestige institutional markers. The topic area is grant-rich: NSF, DARPA, Knight Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, and numerous other funders have active programs. Faculty participation generates publications in high-impact venues.

**Deliverable 2: Longitudinal Community Epistemic Health Study.** A multi-year study tracking information literacy, institutional trust, and vulnerability to manipulation across the university's surrounding community. This provides the empirical baseline against which framework interventions are measured and contributes to a nascent but critically important field of community-level epistemic assessment.

*Incentive alignment:* Longitudinal datasets are high-value academic assets. The study design supports dozens of individual research projects and dissertations.

### 5.2 Education and Curriculum

**Deliverable 3: Epistemic Fitness Curriculum Development.** A cross-disciplinary curriculum initiative developing course modules, pedagogical resources, and assessment instruments for teaching critical evaluation of information in the AI era. Modules are designed for integration into general education requirements, professional school curricula, and community-facing programming.

*Incentive alignment:* Curriculum innovation is valued in promotion and tenure processes. The modules are publishable and licensable to other institutions. First-mover advantage in this space is significant.

**Deliverable 4: Assessment Reform Initiative.** A systematic program to redesign assessment methods across the university, moving away from formats easily completed by AI toward methods that require demonstrated reasoning, process documentation, oral defense, and collaborative problem-solving.

*Incentive alignment:* Positions the university as a leader in educational innovation. Addresses a pain point felt by faculty across all disciplines.

### 5.3 Community Engagement and Extension

**Deliverable 5: Community Epistemic Infrastructure Partnership (CEIP).** A formal partnership structure connecting the university with local schools, public libraries, local government agencies, and regional professional associations. The CEIP serves as the operational vehicle for deploying framework interventions, collecting feedback data, and coordinating cross-institutional response.

*Incentive alignment:* Community engagement metrics are increasingly important for university accreditation and rankings. Extension and outreach programs are fundable through distinct grant mechanisms. The partnership model generates applied research opportunities.

**Deliverable 6: Public Workshop and Training Program.** A structured series of public-facing programs including "Information Self-Defense" workshops (delivered through library partnerships), AI governance literacy sessions for local government officials, and professional development programs for educators and credentialed professionals.

*Incentive alignment:* Visible community impact. Revenue-generating continuing education opportunities. Media coverage and institutional reputation.

### 5.4 Technology Development

**Deliverable 7: Open-Source Institutional Tools.** Development of practical, deployable tools for institutional use: AI content detection systems calibrated for specific institutional contexts (academic submissions, library acquisitions, public comments), provenance verification interfaces accessible to non-technical users, and community-level information environment monitoring dashboards.

*Incentive alignment:* Open-source tool development generates citations, adoption metrics, and grant justifications. Technology transfer opportunities exist for more specialized applications.

### 5.5 Policy and Governance

**Deliverable 8: Model Policy Library.** A curated, regularly updated collection of model policies for each institutional sector: AI procurement standards for local government, research integrity guidelines for academic institutions, acquisition standards for libraries, and ethical practice frameworks for professional associations. Policies are drafted with implementation guidance and designed for direct adoption or local adaptation.

*Incentive alignment:* Policy influence metrics. The model policy library positions the university as the go-to resource for institutional AI governance, generating media coverage, speaking invitations, and consulting opportunities.

**Deliverable 9: Annual State of Epistemic Infrastructure Report.** A comprehensive annual assessment of the health of epistemic infrastructure in the university's region, documenting threats, institutional responses, intervention effectiveness, and emerging challenges. The report serves simultaneously as a research publication, a community resource, and a framework versioning mechanism.

*Incentive alignment:* Flagship publication generating annual media cycles. Provides the structured occasion for the framework's strategic-level adaptation review.

### 5.6 Institutional Self-Application

**Deliverable 10: University Self-Audit and Reform.** Before positioning itself as a hub for institutional defense, the university must credibly demonstrate that it is addressing generative AI challenges within its own operations — admissions processes, research integrity, credentialing, governance. This deliverable is both a necessary foundation for external credibility and an opportunity to develop and test interventions internally before scaling.

*Incentive alignment:* Risk management. Competitive differentiation. Demonstration of institutional seriousness.

## 6. Discussion: Limitations, Risks, and the Incomplete Starting Point

### 6.1 What This Framework Is Not

This framework is not a solution. It is a structured starting point for developing solutions through iterative, evidence-based practice. The authors are acutely aware that the complexity of the problem exceeds the current state of knowledge, and we present this work in the spirit of operational urgency tempered by epistemic humility.

Specific limitations include:

- **Empirical gaps.** Much of the evidence base for the threat landscape is recent and, in some cases, not yet peer-reviewed (e.g., Kosmyna et al., 2025). The framework's assumptions will need to be revised as the evidence matures.
- **Scalability uncertainty.** The framework is designed for implementation by a single university and its surrounding institutional ecosystem. Whether it scales effectively to larger geographies or different institutional contexts is an open question.
- **Incentive fragility.** The alignment between framework deliverables and university incentive structures is genuine but not guaranteed. Shifts in funding priorities, leadership changes, or competing institutional demands could undermine sustained commitment.
- **Adversarial adaptation.** The actors generating synthetic noise are themselves adaptive. Any detection or defense mechanism will face counter-adaptation. The framework's self-improvement architecture is designed to address this, but the pace of adversarial evolution may exceed the pace of institutional response.
- **Measurement difficulty.** Epistemic infrastructure health is not easily quantified. Developing valid, reliable metrics for community-level epistemic resilience is itself a significant research challenge.

### 6.2 The Incentive Alignment Problem

We must directly acknowledge the structural tension identified in Section 1: the broader economic incentives in the information ecosystem currently favor noise production over noise reduction. Platforms profit from engagement regardless of content quality. AI-generated content that drives clicks is economically indistinguishable from authentic content. Verification is a cost center, not a revenue source.

This framework does not resolve this structural tension at the macroeconomic level. What it does is identify and exploit the *institutional* incentive structures that run counter to the market incentive: universities are rewarded for research impact, educational innovation, and community engagement; libraries are rewarded for patron trust and community service; governments are rewarded (at least in theory) for legitimate democratic function; professional bodies are rewarded for maintaining standards that employers and the public trust.

The bet underlying this framework is that institutional incentives, while less powerful than market incentives in the short term, are more durable and more aligned with epistemic resilience. Whether this bet pays off at sufficient scale and speed to matter remains to be determined.

### 6.3 The Urgency-Rigor Tension

Academic frameworks demand rigor. The epistemic crisis demands speed. These are in tension. The framework's versioning and adaptation architecture is an attempt to navigate this tension — to deploy interventions based on the best current evidence while maintaining the scholarly infrastructure to correct course as better evidence emerges. We explicitly prefer action under uncertainty to paralysis pending certainty, while building in the mechanisms to catch and correct errors.

---

## 7. Conclusion: An Invitation to Lead

The degradation of epistemic infrastructure is not a future risk; it is a present reality. AI-generated content has already overtaken human-created content online. Academic publishing is already contaminated. Democratic input mechanisms are already vulnerable. Cognitive capacities are already being eroded by habitual AI delegation. The institutions that could defend against these developments are already confused and reactive.

A university that chooses to lead in this space — to build the research center, develop the curricula, forge the community partnerships, deploy the tools, publish the findings, and iterate relentlessly — will not only serve a profound public need. It will position itself at the center of one of the defining intellectual and practical challenges of the coming decades. The work is fundable, publishable, impactful, and urgent. The competition, for now, is thin.

The grifters are counting on institutional paralysis. The question is whether any institution will move decisively enough to prove them wrong.

This framework is version 1.0. It is incomplete by design and by necessity. We invite collaborators, critics, and co-builders.
